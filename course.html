Course overview

Vision plays an essential role in human perception, so understanding and automating the tasks that rely on it is one of the most consequential scientific challenges today — with applications across industrial automation, autonomous vehicles, precision agriculture, and e-health. Computer Vision is the engineering field that takes on this challenge: extracting a high-level understanding of the world from digital images and videos.

This course is an introduction to the most relevant topics in Computer Vision and their engineering. It deliberately integrates traditional (geometry, optics, filtering, classical detectors) and modern, machine-learning-driven approaches (CNNs, transformers, generative models). The goal is a solid foundation that lets you build sophisticated applications and manage complex CV projects — from how an image is formed and encoded, through how it is processed and analysed, to the deep models that now define the state of the art.

Learning objectives

The course provides a solid basis for building sophisticated applications and managing complex projects in Computer Vision. Students gain a solid understanding of the essence of digital images and videos — from formation and codification to processing and storage — and learn the most relevant problems in CV today together with the best techniques known to address them.

  • Pose, discuss and solve the most common Computer Vision problems, choosing appropriate classical or learned techniques.
  • Fluency in developing Computer Vision applications in Python (OpenCV for classical pipelines, deep-learning frameworks for modern models).
  • Teamwork skills: communication, coordination and leadership, exercised through the group project.
  • Conceptual mastery of the full pipeline: image formation and sensing → geometric & intensity transforms → filtering and frequency analysis → features, segmentation and detection → deep architectures, generative models and transformers.

Program — 30 sessions across 7 modules

Every session below is live and in-person. Session titles and topics follow the official syllabus; explanations, formulas, "key ideas", and demo links are study aids added for this companion site.

Module 1 · Sessions 1–4

Foundations & Computer-Vision Engineering

What computer vision is, where it sits among neighbouring fields, and the physical front end of every system: light, optics, sensors, colour, encoding, and the practical engineering of getting images and video streams into a program. This is the "how an image comes to exist" half of the course's image-formation story.

  • Situate CV relative to image processing, graphics, photogrammetry and machine learning, and recount its historical arc.
  • Explain illumination, optics (including fisheye/omnidirectional lenses) and sensor behaviour and how each shapes the captured image.
  • Reason about colour perception, colour spaces and image compression; take first steps in OpenCV.
  • Configure a real camera (focus, iris, exposure, white balance, gamma) and stream it over the network (RTSP).
SESSION 1 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Course presentation

Objective: define the field, fix the vocabulary, and survey what is and isn't possible today.

  • Definition & relationship with other disciplines — CV recovers semantic/geometric information from images, the inverse of computer graphics (which renders images from a model); it overlaps image processing, photogrammetry, pattern recognition and ML.
  • Main concepts — image as a function $I(x,y)$, the pipeline from acquisition to interpretation, and the notion of an ill-posed inverse problem.
  • Historical background — from Roberts' blocks world (1963) and Marr's levels of analysis to the deep-learning inflection point (AlexNet, 2012).
  • State of the art — detection, segmentation, depth, generation, and large vision-language models framing today's applications.
Key idea: vision is an inverse problem — infer the 3-D world and its meaning from a 2-D projection — which is why priors, geometry and learning all matter.
Demo · Site overview Demo · 1 Image formation
  • Szeliski, Computer Vision: Algorithms and Applications — Ch. 1 (Introduction).
  • Forsyth & Ponce, Computer Vision: A Modern Approach — Ch. 1.
SESSION 2 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Computer Vision Engineering (I)

Objective: understand the optical and illumination physics that determine image quality.

  • Basic illumination techniques & sources — front/back/structured lighting; controlling light is often cheaper than fixing the algorithm.
  • Camera elements & optics fundamentals — aperture, focal length, depth of field; the thin-lens relation $\tfrac{1}{f}=\tfrac{1}{z_o}+\tfrac{1}{z_i}$.
  • Fisheye & omnidirectional cameras — wide-FOV non-perspective projections and the radial distortion they introduce.
  • Sensors & example applications — CCD vs CMOS, the photon→charge→digital path, and where each lens/sensor choice is used.
Thin lens: 1/f = 1/z_o + 1/z_i  ·  FOV ≈ 2·arctan(sensor_size / 2f)
Key idea: the image you analyse is already the product of physics choices — lighting, lens and sensor — made before a single line of code runs.
Demo · 3 Image sensing
  • Szeliski — Ch. 2.1–2.3 (image formation, photometric & optical models).
  • Livingstone, Vision and Art: The Biology of Seeing — light & the eye as biological motivation.
SESSION 3 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Computer Vision Engineering (II)

Objective: represent colour, encode images efficiently, and start coding with OpenCV.

  • Color perception — trichromatic human vision; why three channels suffice and metamerism arises.
  • Color spaces — RGB, HSV, YCbCr, Lab; choosing a space that separates the property you care about (e.g. hue vs brightness).
  • Image compression — redundancy removal; JPEG's block DCT + quantization + entropy coding (lossy) vs PNG (lossless).
  • First steps with OpenCV — reading/displaying images, channel order (BGR), data types, basic manipulation.
Key idea: JPEG already is a frequency-domain idea — it throws away high-frequency detail your eye barely notices, exactly the Fourier intuition from Module 3.
Demo · 5 Intensity & histogram Demo · Image input
  • Szeliski — Ch. 2.3 (colour) & Ch. 2.3.3 (compression).
  • Forsyth & Ponce — colour chapter.
SESSION 4 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Camera configuration & streaming

Objective: operate a real camera and deliver a live video stream — the engineering glue of a CV system.

  • Acquisition methods — frame grabbing, triggering, and synchronisation.
  • Focus / Iris — sharpness and the aperture's trade-off between light intake and depth of field.
  • Exposure, white balance, gamma — exposure time vs gain (noise); white balance for colour constancy; gamma encoding $V_{out}=V_{in}^{\gamma}$ matching perceptual response.
  • Setting up an RTSP server — packaging frames into a real-time streaming protocol for networked consumption.
Gamma: V_out = V_in ^ γ  (encode ~1/2.2, decode ~2.2)
Key idea: exposure, white balance and gamma are point intensity transforms applied in the camera — the same maps you'll implement by hand in Session 15.
Demo · 5 Intensity (gamma/stretch)
  • Szeliski — Ch. 2.3.1 (sampling, aliasing, exposure) & OpenCV video I/O docs.
Module 2 · Sessions 5–13

Geometry, 3-D Vision & Calibration

The geometric backbone of computer vision: projective spaces, image transformations, the camera projection model, recovering 3-D structure from one or many views, depth sensing, and calibrating real cameras. This is where pixels become geometry.

  • Work in projective spaces and homogeneous coordinates, and apply the 2-D transformation hierarchy (rigid → affine → projective).
  • Write the full camera model $x = K\,[R\,|\,t]\,X$ and reason about intrinsics, extrinsics and coordinate frames.
  • Recover depth and 3-D structure via stereo, structured light, time-of-flight, optical flow, homography and epipolar geometry.
  • Calibrate a camera with Zhang's method and judge calibration quality by reprojection error.
SESSION 5 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Introduction to projective geometry

Objective: adopt homogeneous coordinates so projection and perspective become linear.

  • Projective space ℙⁿ — points as rays through the origin; equivalence up to scale, with points at infinity made explicit.
  • Projective space ℙ² — the image plane: points and lines are dual 3-vectors; their cross product gives intersection/join.
  • Projective space ℙ³ — the world space whose points the camera projects down to ℙ².
Homogeneous: (x, y) ↔ (x, y, 1) ~ (λx, λy, λ), λ ≠ 0
Key idea: moving to homogeneous coordinates turns the non-linear perspective division into a single matrix multiply — the trick that powers the whole geometry module.
Demo · 2 Camera model
  • Hartley & Zisserman, Multiple View Geometry — Ch. 1–2 (projective geometry of 2-D/3-D).
SESSION 6 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Image transformations

Objective: classify and apply the 2-D transformation hierarchy on images.

  • Scaling & Translation — resizing and shifting; translation needs the homogeneous extra row to be linear.
  • Rotation — orthonormal $R$ preserving lengths and angles (rigid/Euclidean).
  • Affine — 6-DOF map preserving parallelism (rotation + scale + shear + translation).
  • Perspective — 8-DOF homography preserving straight lines but not parallelism; models a plane viewed from any angle.
Affine: [x'; y'; 1] = [[a,b,t_x],[c,d,t_y],[0,0,1]] · [x; y; 1]
Key idea: each transform is a strictly larger group than the last (rigid ⊂ similarity ⊂ affine ⊂ projective); the DOF count tells you how many point correspondences you need to estimate it.
Demo · 4 Geometric transforms
  • Szeliski — Ch. 2.1.1 & 3.6 (2-D transforms, warping).
  • Hartley & Zisserman — Ch. 2 (the transformation hierarchy).
SESSION 7 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Image acquisition model

Objective: write the complete pixel-from-world projection equation.

  • Lens models: pinhole, thin, thick — ideal pinhole projection plus the corrections real lenses require.
  • Projection models — perspective vs orthographic/weak-perspective approximations.
  • Intrinsic & extrinsic parameters — $K$ (focal lengths $f_x,f_y$, principal point $c_x,c_y$, skew) vs $[R\,|\,t]$ (camera pose).
  • Coordinate systems involved — world → camera → image → pixel, and the transform chaining them.
x = K [R | t] X  ,  K = [[f_x, s, c_x],[0, f_y, c_y],[0,0,1]]  ·  pinhole: x = f·X/Z
Key idea: the camera matrix factorises cleanly into what the camera is (intrinsics $K$) and where it is (extrinsics $[R|t]$) — calibration estimates the first, pose estimation the second.
Demo · 1 Pinhole projection Demo · 2 K[R|t]
  • Hartley & Zisserman — Ch. 6 (camera models).
  • Forsyth & Ponce — geometric camera models.
SESSION 8 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Three-dimensional vision I & II

Objective: survey the cues and devices that recover depth from images.

  • Human 3-D & stereoscopic vision — binocular disparity; depth $Z = f\,B / d$ from baseline $B$ and disparity $d$.
  • Structured light (projection) — projecting a known pattern and decoding its deformation for dense depth.
  • Time of flight — measuring light round-trip time, $Z = c\,\Delta t / 2$.
  • Depth from focus / shape from shading & texture — using blur, shading gradients and texture foreshortening as monocular depth cues.
  • Optical flow & motion — the brightness-constancy constraint $I_x u + I_y v + I_t = 0$ for tracking and motion detection.
Stereo depth: Z = f·B / d  ·  Optical flow: I_x·u + I_y·v + I_t = 0
Key idea: there are many independent paths to depth — geometry (stereo), active sensing (structured light, ToF) and image cues (focus, shading, motion); robust systems fuse several.
Demo · 8 Gradients (flow building block)
  • Szeliski — Ch. 11–12 (stereo, 3-D reconstruction) & Ch. 9 (motion/optical flow).
  • Hartley & Zisserman — stereo & reconstruction.
SESSION 9 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Multiple view geometry

Objective: relate two or more views algebraically.

  • Single & dual-camera models — one projection matrix vs a stereo pair sharing a rigid relative pose.
  • Homography — a 3×3 $H$ mapping one image of a plane to another (also pure-rotation panoramas).
  • Epipolar geometry — the fundamental matrix $F$ with $x'^{\!\top} F x = 0$ constraining a point to a line in the other view.
  • Structure from motion — jointly recovering cameras and 3-D points from many views.
Epipolar constraint: x'ᵀ F x = 0  ·  Planar map: x' = H x
Key idea: the epipolar constraint collapses a 2-D correspondence search to a 1-D line search — the single most useful geometric fact in stereo and SfM.
Demo · 4 Projective/homography Demo · 14 Matching (correspondence)
  • Hartley & Zisserman — Ch. 9–11 (epipolar geometry, F, trifocal & SfM).
SESSION 10 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Multiple view geometry (continued)

Objective: deepen the two-view tools and complete the SfM pipeline.

  • Single & dual-camera models — revisited with estimation in practice (normalised coordinates, RANSAC robustness).
  • Homography — DLT estimation from ≥ 4 correspondences and its applications (rectification, mosaicing).
  • Epipolar geometry — estimating $F$/$E$ (8-point algorithm) and decomposing $E$ into relative $R, t$.
  • Structure from motion — triangulation + bundle adjustment refining cameras and points jointly.
Essential: E = [t]_× R  ·  F = K'⁻ᵀ E K⁻¹
Key idea: bundle adjustment is the workhorse optimiser of 3-D vision — it minimises total reprojection error over all cameras and points at once.
Demo · 14 Template matching
  • Hartley & Zisserman — Ch. 11 (estimating F) & Appendix on bundle adjustment.
  • Szeliski — Ch. 11 (SfM).
SESSION 11 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Depth cameras

Objective: configure and capture from real depth-sensing rigs.

  • Stereoscopic camera configuration & capture — calibrating/rectifying a stereo pair so disparity maps directly to depth.
  • Structured-light camera configuration & capture — projector–camera setup, pattern choice, and decoding to a depth map.
Key idea: a rectified stereo pair turns the epipolar lines horizontal, so depth becomes a simple per-row 1-D disparity search.
Demo · 2 Camera model
  • Szeliski — Ch. 12 (depth sensing, active illumination).
SESSION 12 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Camera calibration I

Objective: understand how intrinsics and distortion are estimated.

  • Introduction & tools — why calibration matters and the OpenCV calibration toolchain.
  • Zhang's method — a planar checkerboard at several poses yields homographies that constrain $K$ and lens distortion.
  • Calibration process issues — pattern coverage, degenerate poses, and distortion-model selection.
Key idea: Zhang's flexible method needs only a printed checkerboard waved at the camera — each planar view gives a homography that constrains the unknown intrinsics.
Demo · 2 Intrinsics K
  • Zhang (2000), "A flexible new technique for camera calibration"; Szeliski Ch. 6.3.
SESSION 13 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Camera calibration II

Objective: run an end-to-end calibration and validate it.

  • Pattern image acquisition — capturing well-distributed checkerboard views.
  • Running the calibration process — detecting corners, solving for $K$, distortion and per-view poses.
  • Checking reprojection error — the RMS pixel error between detected and reprojected corners as the quality metric.
Reprojection error = (1/N) Σ ‖ x_i − project(K, R, t, X_i) ‖
Key idea: reprojection error in sub-pixel units is the honest yardstick — a calibration is only as good as the geometry it reproduces.
Demo · 2 Reprojection intuition
  • Szeliski — Ch. 6.3 (calibration practice); OpenCV calibration tutorial.

Module 4 · Sessions 15–17

Image Processing — Point, Local & Global Operations

Classical image processing organised by spatial scope: pixelwise (point) operations, neighbourhood (local/convolution) operations, and whole-image (global/Fourier) operations. These are the operators every later technique is built from.

  • Apply point transforms (negatives, log, gamma, contrast stretch, histogram equalization, binarization) and read histograms.
  • Design and apply linear filters via convolution, exploit separability, and use orientable/band-pass filters.
  • Analyse images in the frequency domain with the 2-D DFT and interpret filtering as spectrum shaping.
SESSION 15 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Pixelwise operations

Objective: transform each pixel independently via an intensity map $s = T(r)$.

  • Pixelwise operations & pixel transformations — negative $s=L-1-r$, log $s=c\log(1+r)$, gamma $s=c\,r^{\gamma}$, contrast stretching.
  • Histogram equalization — remapping intensities by the CDF to flatten the histogram and maximise global contrast.
  • Binarization — thresholding to a 0/1 image, the gateway to morphology and connected components.
s = T(r)  ·  equalize: s = round((L−1)·cdf(r))  ·  gamma: s = c·rᵞ
Key idea: a point operation depends only on a pixel's own value, so it is fully described by a 1-D lookup curve — and the histogram tells you which curve helps.
Demo · 5 Intensity & histogram Demo · 9 Binarization
  • Szeliski — Ch. 3.1 (point operators) & 3.1.4 (histogram equalization).
  • Forsyth & Ponce — early image-processing chapter.
SESSION 16 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Local operations

Objective: process each pixel using its neighbourhood via convolution.

  • Introduction — neighbourhood operators and the convolution definition.
  • Separable filters — a 2-D Gaussian factorises into two 1-D passes, cutting cost from $O(k^2)$ to $O(2k)$ per pixel.
  • Linear filtering examples — box/Gaussian blur, Sobel/Laplacian edges, sharpening.
  • Band-pass & orientable filters; other local operators — DoG band-pass, steerable filters, and non-linear ops (median, bilateral).
(I * K)(x,y) = Σ_i Σ_j I(x−i, y−j) · K(i,j)
Key idea: convolution is the atom of classical vision and of CNNs — the only thing that changes later is that the kernel weights are learned rather than designed.
Demo · 6 Convolution playground Demo · 8 Sobel/DoG Demo · 11 CNN (learned kernels)
  • Szeliski — Ch. 3.2 (linear filtering, separability) & 3.3 (non-linear filters).
SESSION 17 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Global operations

Objective: analyse and filter images in the frequency domain.

  • Fourier transform & the 2-D (bidimensional) DFT — decomposing an image into spatial-frequency components.
  • DFT interpretation — magnitude (how much of each frequency) vs phase (where structure sits); low frequencies = smooth regions, high = edges/texture.
  • Other global transformations — DCT (compression), wavelets (multi-scale).
F(u,v) = Σ_x Σ_y I(x,y) · e^( −j2π (ux/M + vy/N) )
Key idea: by the convolution theorem, blurring/sharpening in space is just multiplying the spectrum by a low-/high-pass mask — the same operation seen from the other domain.
Demo · 7 2-D DFT & filtering
  • Szeliski — Ch. 3.4 (Fourier transforms) & 3.5 (pyramids/wavelets).
  • Forsyth & Ponce — frequency-domain chapter.
Module 5 · Sessions 18–20

Classical Features, Recognition & Matching

Detecting salient structure (corners, edges, blobs), segmenting and recognising objects with classical pipelines, and matching images via templates and feature correspondences + homography — the pre-deep-learning toolkit that still underpins SLAM, panoramas and registration.

  • Detect corners (Harris & Stephens), edges and curvature-based salient features.
  • Approach object recognition through edges, thresholding and region-based segmentation.
  • Match images with template matching and with feature correspondences + homography estimation.
SESSION 18 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Salient feature detection

Objective: find repeatable, distinctive image points.

  • Introduction & definitions — what makes a feature "good": repeatability, distinctiveness, locality, invariance.
  • Surface curvature & curvature-based methods — corners as points of high curvature in the intensity surface.
  • Harris & Stephens method — the second-moment matrix $M$; cornerness $R = \det(M) - k\,\mathrm{tr}(M)^2$.
  • Gradient-based methods — building detectors/descriptors from image gradients $\nabla I = (I_x, I_y)$.
M = Σ_w [[I_x², I_xI_y],[I_xI_y, I_y²]]  ·  R = det(M) − k·tr(M)²
Key idea: a corner is where intensity changes in two directions, i.e. both eigenvalues of the local gradient covariance $M$ are large — Harris just scores that cheaply.
Demo · 8 Harris / DoG / Canny
  • Harris & Stephens (1988); Szeliski Ch. 7.1 (feature detectors).
SESSION 19 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Intro to object recognition

Objective: separate objects from background with classical segmentation.

  • Introduction — recognition vs detection vs segmentation framed.
  • Edge detection-based techniques — closing edge maps into object boundaries.
  • Image thresholding — global/adaptive thresholds; Otsu maximising between-class variance to pick the level automatically.
  • Region-based algorithms — region growing, split-and-merge, connected components.
Otsu: t* = argmax_t σ²_between(t) = argmax_t w₀w₁(μ₀−μ₁)²
Key idea: segmentation can be driven by discontinuity (edges) or similarity (regions) — two complementary views of the same partition problem.
Demo · 13 Otsu / region / edge / k-means Demo · 9 Connected components
  • Otsu (1979); Szeliski Ch. 5 (segmentation); Forsyth & Ponce segmentation chapters.
SESSION 20 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Image matching techniques

Objective: locate and align content across images.

  • Template matching — sliding a patch and scoring with SSD or normalised cross-correlation (NCC), robust to brightness changes.
  • Feature matching + homography — matching descriptors, rejecting outliers with RANSAC, then fitting $H$ to align/stitch.
NCC(u,v) = Σ (I−Ī)(T−T̄) / sqrt( Σ(I−Ī)² · Σ(T−T̄)² )
Key idea: template matching is brittle to rotation/scale; feature matching + RANSAC + homography is the robust upgrade that makes panoramas and registration work.
Demo · 14 NCC / SSD matching Demo · 4 Homography warp
  • Szeliski — Ch. 7.1–7.2 (matching) & Ch. 8 (image alignment/stitching).
Module 6 · Sessions 21–24

Deep Learning for Vision — CNNs & Architectures

The deep-learning core: convolutional neural networks from first principles, implementation in modern frameworks, the canonical architecture lineage (AlexNet → VGG → GoogLeNet → ResNet → U-Net), and recurrent/attention models for sequence and captioning tasks.

  • Explain CNN building blocks — convolution, non-linearity, pooling — and why they suit images.
  • Implement a CNN from scratch and in a deep-learning framework.
  • Compare landmark architectures and the ideas they introduced (depth, 1×1 convs, residuals, encoder–decoder).
  • Describe RNN/GRU/LSTM models and attention-based image captioning.
SESSION 21 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Convolutional neural networks

Objective: motivate and define the CNN.

  • Introduction — from the MLP (fully-connected, ignores spatial structure) to weight-shared convolutions.
  • Applications — classification, detection, segmentation, and beyond.
  • Functional perspective — a CNN as a composition of layers; learning by gradient descent through backprop.
conv: y = σ(W * x + b)  ·  out = (n + 2p − k)/s + 1
Key idea: convolution gives CNNs local connectivity, weight sharing and translation equivariance — far fewer parameters than a dense net, and the right inductive bias for images.
Demo · 10 MLP playground Demo · 11 CNN feature maps
  • Drummy et al., Dive into Deep Learning — CNN chapters; Szeliski Ch. 5.3–5.4.
SESSION 22 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Convolutional neural networks II

Objective: build a CNN in practice.

  • Deep learning frameworks — tensors, autograd, the train loop (forward → loss → backward → step), GPUs.
  • Implementing a CNN from scratch — stacking conv/activation/pool/FC layers, choosing loss and optimiser, and training.
max-pool 2×2: y(i,j) = max over 2×2 window  ·  ReLU: σ(z)=max(0,z)
Key idea: pooling and strided convolution shrink spatial resolution while growing channel depth — the network trades "where" for "what" as it goes deeper.
Demo · 11 Pooling & activations
  • Dive into Deep Learning — modern training; framework tutorials (PyTorch).
SESSION 23 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Convolutional neural networks III — advanced architectures

Objective: trace the landmark architectures and the ideas each contributed.

  • AlexNet — the 2012 ImageNet breakthrough: ReLU, dropout, GPU training at scale.
  • VGG — depth via uniform stacks of small 3×3 convolutions.
  • GoogLeNet — Inception modules and 1×1 convolutions for cheap channel mixing.
  • ResNet — residual connections $y = F(x) + x$ enabling very deep networks.
  • U-Net — symmetric encoder–decoder with skip connections for pixel-wise (segmentation) output.
Residual block: y = F(x, {W_i}) + x
Key idea: the residual shortcut lets gradients flow straight through, so "deeper" stops hurting — ResNet is why 100+ layer networks train at all.
Demo · 12 LeNet/VGG/ResNet/U-Net/ViT
  • He et al. (ResNet, 2016); Simonyan & Zisserman (VGG); Dive into Deep Learning — modern CNNs.
SESSION 24 · LIVE IN-PERSON

Recurrent neural networks

Objective: model sequences and connect vision to language.

  • Introduction — recurrence $h_t = f(h_{t-1}, x_t)$ for variable-length sequences (e.g. video, captions).
  • GRU networks — gated units mitigating vanishing gradients with fewer parameters than LSTM.
  • LSTM networks — cell state plus input/forget/output gates for long-range dependencies.
  • Image captioning with attention — a CNN encoder + RNN decoder that attends to image regions while generating words.
h_t = f(W·x_t + U·h_{t−1} + b)  ·  attention: α = softmax(score(h, features))
Key idea: attention lets the decoder look back at the most relevant image regions for each word — the precursor of the transformer attention in Session 29.

Detection & segmentation using Deep Learning

Objective: map each dense-prediction task to its network design.

  • Classification + localization — predicting a class plus a bounding box (classification + regression heads).

Generative models II

Objective: contrast adversarial and diffusion generation.

  • Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) — a generator vs discriminator min-max game producing sharp samples (with mode-collapse risk).
  • Diffusion models — learning to reverse a gradual noising process; denoise from pure noise to a sample (the basis of modern image generators).
GAN: min_G max_D E[log D(x)] + E[log(1 − D(G(z)))]  ·  Diffusion: x_t = √(ᾱ_t) x₀ + √(1−ᾱ_t) ε
Key idea: GANs learn generation implicitly through a critic; diffusion learns it by reversing noise step by step — more stable to train and now state of the art.

Transformer architecture

Objective: understand attention and Vision Transformers.

  • Self-attention & transformers — relating all positions via scaled dot-product attention; no recurrence or convolution required.
  • Vision Transformers (ViT) — split the image into patches, embed them as tokens, and process with a transformer encoder.
  • Hybrid models — combining CNN feature extractors with transformer reasoning (e.g. DETR).
  • Applications — classification, detection and generation under one attention-based umbrella.
Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax( Q Kᵀ / √d_k ) V
Key idea: self-attention gives a global receptive field in one layer — every patch can attend to every other — which is what convolution achieves only by stacking depth.

Key concepts — glossary

Roughly 30 terms threaded through the course, in pipeline order. Each links conceptually to a session and, where relevant, a live demo.

Homogeneous coordinates
Appending a scale coordinate so points are defined up to a non-zero factor; makes translation and perspective linear matrix operations. (S5)
Pinhole camera
Ideal projection $x=f X/Z,\,y=fY/Z$; the geometric core of every camera model. (S2, S7)
Intrinsics K
Focal lengths, principal point and skew — the camera's internal geometry estimated by calibration. (S7, S12–13)
Extrinsics [R|t]
The camera's rotation and translation in the world — its pose. (S7)
Homography
A 3×3 projective map between two images of a plane (or pure-rotation views); 8 DOF. (S6, S9–10, S20)
Epipolar geometry
The two-view constraint $x'^{\top}Fx=0$ that reduces correspondence search to a line. (S9–10)
Disparity
Horizontal shift of a point between rectified stereo views; inversely proportional to depth. (S8, S11)
Structure from motion
Recovering cameras and 3-D points from multiple views, refined by bundle adjustment. (S9–10)
Optical flow
Apparent per-pixel motion field, constrained by brightness constancy $I_xu+I_yv+I_t=0$. (S8)
Camera calibration
Estimating $K$ and lens distortion (Zhang's method), validated by reprojection error. (S12–13)
Reprojection error
RMS pixel distance between observed and reprojected points — the calibration quality metric. (S13)
Color space
A coordinate system for colour (RGB, HSV, Lab, YCbCr) chosen to separate the property of interest. (S3)
Histogram equalization
Remapping intensities by their CDF to maximise contrast. (S15, demo 5)
Convolution
Sliding weighted sum $(I*K)(x,y)=\sum I(x-i,y-j)K(i,j)$; the atom of filtering and CNNs. (S16, demo 6)
Separable filter
A 2-D kernel that factors into two 1-D passes, cutting cost from $O(k^2)$ to $O(2k)$. (S16)
Image gradient
$\nabla I=(I_x,I_y)$; magnitude marks edges, orientation their direction. (S8, S16, S18)
Edge detection
Locating intensity discontinuities (Sobel magnitude, Canny with NMS + hysteresis). (S16, S18–19, demo 8)
Fourier transform (2-D DFT)
Image as a sum of spatial frequencies; filtering = shaping the spectrum. (S17, demo 7)
Harris corner
Cornerness $R=\det(M)-k\,\mathrm{tr}(M)^2$ from the gradient second-moment matrix. (S18, demo 8)
Otsu thresholding
Automatic binarization maximising between-class variance. (S19, demo 13)
Morphology
Erode/dilate/open/close on binary images via a structuring element. (S15, demo 9)
NCC / template matching
Brightness-invariant patch correlation for locating templates. (S20, demo 14)
Pooling
Down-sampling (max/avg) that adds translation tolerance and shrinks feature maps. (S22, demo 11)
Receptive field
The input region influencing one activation; grows with depth, instant with attention. (S21–23, S29)
Residual connection
$y=F(x)+x$ shortcut that lets very deep networks train (ResNet). (S23, demo 12)
Encoder–decoder (U-Net)
Contracting + expanding paths with skip connections for dense (segmentation) output. (S23, S25, demo 12)
IoU & NMS
Intersection-over-union overlap metric and non-max suppression to prune duplicate detections. (S25–26, demo 14)
Latent space
Low-dimensional code an autoencoder/VAE maps to; smooth in VAEs so interpolation is meaningful. (S27, demos 16–17)
GAN
Generator vs discriminator min-max game producing sharp samples. (S28, demo 17)
Diffusion model
Generation by learning to reverse a gradual noising process. (S28, demo 17)
Self-attention
$\mathrm{softmax}(QK^{\top}/\sqrt{d_k})V$ relating all positions; the basis of transformers/ViT. (S29, demo 12)
Vision Transformer (ViT)
Image as a sequence of patch tokens processed by a transformer encoder. (S29, demo 12)
